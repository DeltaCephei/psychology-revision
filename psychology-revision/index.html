Topic Summaries

Psychology uses six major approaches to explain why people think, feel, and behave the way they do. Each approach offers a different lens ‚Äî from biology and conditioning to culture and the unconscious mind. Click on each one below to explore its key ideas, strengths, limitations, and debates.

Use the coloured circles on each card to self-assess your confidence: ● not confident, ● somewhat confident, ● confident. This helps you see at a glance which approaches need more work ‚Äî research shows that identifying your weak spots and focusing study time there (metacognition) is one of the most effective revision strategies (Dunlosky et al., 2013).

Biological Approach

Level 2 Focus

Key Idea: Behaviour and mental processes are shaped by our biology - our brain, nervous system, hormones, and genetics.

Key Concepts:
  • Brain structure: Different brain regions control different functions (e.g., amygdala for emotion, prefrontal cortex for decision-making)
  • Neurotransmitters: Chemical messengers like serotonin (mood), dopamine (reward), and adrenaline (fight-or-flight)
  • Genetics: Inherited traits can influence behaviour and predisposition to conditions
Strengths:
  • Scientific and objective - can measure biological factors
  • Has led to effective treatments (e.g., medication for depression)
Limitations:
  • Reductionist - reduces complex behaviour to biology alone
  • Ignores environmental and social influences
Key Debates:
  • Nature vs nurture: Twin studies show high heritability for traits like schizophrenia (~48% concordance in identical twins), but the gap from 100% proves environment also matters
  • Biological determinism: Are we controlled by our biology? The MAOA "warrior gene" controversy showed the dangers of reducing complex behaviour to a single gene
  • Reductionism: Explaining depression solely as low serotonin is biologically reductionist ‚Äî it ignores social and psychological factors
  • Diathesis-stress model: An interactionist resolution ‚Äî disorders need both a genetic vulnerability AND an environmental trigger
  • Epigenetics: Environment can switch genes on/off without changing DNA, showing nature and nurture constantly interact

Level 3 Focus

Key Idea: Advanced understanding of how biological mechanisms interact with environment to produce behaviour.

Key Concepts:
  • Neuroplasticity: The brain's ability to reorganise and form new neural connections throughout life
  • Epigenetics: How environmental factors can switch genes on/off without changing DNA
  • Localisation of function: Specific brain areas for specific functions (Broca's area, Wernicke's area)
  • Split-brain research: Sperry's studies on hemispheric specialisation
Key Studies:
  • Phineas Gage (1848): Brain damage affecting personality
  • HM case study: Role of hippocampus in memory formation
Critical Evaluation:
  • Nature vs nurture debate - interaction between genes and environment
  • Ethical issues with biological interventions
  • Deterministic implications - are we controlled by biology?

Behaviourist Approach

Level 2 Focus

Key Idea: All behaviour is learned from the environment through conditioning. We are born as a "blank slate" (tabula rasa).

Key Concepts:
  • Classical Conditioning (Pavlov): Learning through association - pairing a neutral stimulus with an unconditioned stimulus
  • Operant Conditioning (Skinner): Learning through consequences - reinforcement and punishment
  • Positive reinforcement: Adding something pleasant to increase behaviour
  • Negative reinforcement: Removing something unpleasant to increase behaviour
  • Punishment: Adding something unpleasant or removing something pleasant to decrease behaviour
Strengths:
  • Scientific approach - controlled lab experiments
  • Practical applications (therapy, education, training)
Limitations:
  • Ignores internal mental processes (thoughts, feelings)
  • Animal research may not apply to humans
  • Environmentally deterministic
Key Debates:
  • Environmental determinism: Skinner argued free will is an illusion ‚Äî all behaviour is shaped by reinforcement history. Even "free choices" trace back to environmental causes
  • Nurture position: Behaviourists take the extreme nurture side (tabula rasa). Watson claimed he could train any infant to become any specialist regardless of ancestry
  • Environmental reductionism: Reduces all behaviour to simple stimulus-response links, ignoring biology, cognition, and social context
  • Applications: Systematic desensitisation, token economies, and ABA therapy show how conditioning principles treat real-world problems

Level 3 Focus

Key Idea: Deeper analysis of learning theory and its applications, including social learning.

Key Concepts:
  • Social Learning Theory (Bandura): Learning through observation and imitation
  • Vicarious reinforcement: Learning from observing others being rewarded/punished
  • Self-efficacy: Belief in one's ability to succeed
  • Schedules of reinforcement: Continuous vs partial reinforcement
Key Studies:
  • Pavlov's dogs: Classical conditioning demonstration
  • Little Albert (Watson): Conditioned fear response
  • Skinner Box: Operant conditioning research
  • Bobo Doll (Bandura): Observational learning of aggression
Critical Evaluation:
  • Ethical concerns with conditioning experiments
  • Free will vs determinism debate
  • Integration with cognitive approach (cognitive-behavioural)

Cognitive Approach

Level 2 Focus

Key Idea: Behaviour is influenced by internal mental processes - how we think, perceive, remember, and solve problems.

Key Concepts:
  • Information processing: The mind works like a computer (input ‚Üí processing ‚Üí output)
  • Schemas: Mental frameworks that help organise and interpret information
  • Memory models: How information is encoded, stored, and retrieved
  • Cognitive biases: Systematic errors in thinking
Strengths:
  • Focuses on internal processes behaviourism ignores
  • Scientific methods (experiments, brain imaging)
  • Useful applications (CBT therapy, education)
Limitations:
  • Computer analogy may oversimplify the mind
  • Often ignores emotion and social factors
  • Difficult to directly observe mental processes
Key Debates:
  • Machine reductionism: The computer analogy reduces the mind to information processing, ignoring emotion, motivation, and social context
  • System 1 vs System 2: Kahneman showed humans rely on fast, automatic thinking (System 1) that is prone to biases like confirmation bias and the availability heuristic
  • False memories: Loftus demonstrated that memories can be implanted ‚Äî 25% of participants in the "Lost in the Mall" study believed a fabricated event was real
  • Replication crisis: The Open Science Collaboration (2015) found only ~36% of psychology studies replicated successfully, raising questions about the reliability of cognitive research

Level 3 Focus

Key Idea: Advanced cognitive models and their application to understanding complex behaviour.

Key Concepts:
  • Multi-store memory model (Atkinson & Shiffrin): Sensory ‚Üí Short-term ‚Üí Long-term memory
  • Working memory model (Baddeley): Central executive, phonological loop, visuospatial sketchpad
  • Reconstructive memory (Bartlett): Memory is actively rebuilt, not passively replayed
  • Cognitive distortions: Faulty thinking patterns (catastrophising, black-and-white thinking)
Key Studies:
  • Loftus & Palmer: Eyewitness testimony and leading questions
  • Bartlett's "War of the Ghosts": Schema influence on memory
Critical Evaluation:
  • Ecological validity of lab experiments
  • Individual differences in cognitive processing
  • Integration with neuroscience (cognitive neuroscience)

Humanistic Approach

Level 2 Focus

Key Idea: Humans have free will and an innate drive toward personal growth and self-actualisation.

Key Concepts:
  • Self-actualisation: Reaching your full potential
  • Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs: Basic needs must be met before higher needs (physiological ‚Üí safety ‚Üí love ‚Üí esteem ‚Üí self-actualisation)
  • Free will: We choose our own behaviour; not determined by biology or environment
  • Self-concept: How you see yourself
Strengths:
  • Positive focus on growth and potential
  • Treats people as individuals, not just data
  • Influential in counselling and therapy
Limitations:
  • Difficult to test scientifically
  • Concepts are vague and subjective
  • May be culturally biased (Western, individualistic)
Key Debates:
  • Free will: The strongest advocate for free will among the six approaches ‚Äî argues that personal agency is essential for dignity and self-actualisation
  • Holism: Studies the whole person rather than breaking behaviour into parts. Rejects biological and environmental reductionism
  • Self-Determination Theory: Deci and Ryan identified competence, autonomy, and relatedness as universal needs for well-being
  • Locus of control: Rotter's concept ‚Äî internal locus (believing you control your fate) is linked to better achievement and coping

Level 3 Focus

Key Idea: In-depth understanding of humanistic principles and their therapeutic applications.

Key Concepts:
  • Rogers' Person-Centred Therapy: Unconditional positive regard, empathy, congruence
  • Conditions of worth: When love/acceptance depends on meeting others' expectations
  • Congruence vs incongruence: Match between ideal self and actual self
  • Peak experiences (Maslow): Moments of profound happiness and fulfilment
Key Theorists:
  • Abraham Maslow: Hierarchy of needs, self-actualisation
  • Carl Rogers: Client-centred therapy, self-concept
Critical Evaluation:
  • Idiographic vs nomothetic approach debate
  • Cultural variations in self-actualisation
  • Limited empirical evidence but strong therapeutic impact

Psychodynamic Approach

Level 2 Focus

Key Idea: Behaviour is influenced by unconscious thoughts, feelings, and childhood experiences.

Key Concepts:
  • The unconscious mind: Thoughts and memories we're not aware of that influence behaviour
  • Structure of personality: Id (pleasure), Ego (reality), Superego (morality)
  • Defence mechanisms: Unconscious strategies to protect the ego (denial, repression, projection)
  • Childhood experiences: Early relationships shape adult personality
Strengths:
  • First to emphasise importance of childhood and unconscious
  • Led to development of talking therapies
  • Rich, detailed case studies
Limitations:
  • Difficult to test scientifically
  • Based mainly on case studies (not generalisable)
  • Overemphasis on sexuality and childhood
Key Debates:
  • Psychic determinism: Freud argued no behaviour is accidental ‚Äî even slips of the tongue reveal unconscious causes. This is a hard determinist position
  • Dual instinct theory: Eros (life/libido) and Thanatos (death/aggression) are two opposing biological drives that shape all behaviour
  • Nature-nurture interaction: Innate biological instincts (nature) are shaped by psychosexual development and family relationships (nurture)
  • Both reductionist and holistic: Reduces behaviour to unconscious drives (reductionist) but also explores complex interactions between id, ego, and superego (holistic)

Level 3 Focus

Key Idea: Advanced understanding of psychodynamic theory and modern developments.

Key Concepts:
  • Psychosexual stages (Freud): Oral, anal, phallic, latency, genital
  • Oedipus/Electra complex: Childhood attraction to opposite-sex parent
  • Attachment theory (Bowlby): Early attachment affects later relationships
  • Transference: Redirecting feelings about parents onto therapist
Key Theorists:
  • Sigmund Freud: Founder of psychoanalysis
  • Anna Freud: Child psychoanalysis, defence mechanisms
  • Erik Erikson: Psychosocial development stages
Critical Evaluation:
  • Unfalsifiable concepts (can't be proven wrong)
  • Gender bias in original theories
  • Modern psychodynamic therapy is more evidence-based

Sociocultural Approach

Level 2 Focus

Key Idea: Behaviour is shaped by social and cultural factors - the groups we belong to and the society we live in.

Key Concepts:
  • Social influence: How others affect our thoughts, feelings, and behaviour
  • Conformity: Changing behaviour to fit in with a group
  • Culture: Shared beliefs, values, and practices of a group
  • Social identity: Part of self-concept based on group membership
Strengths:
  • Recognises importance of social context
  • Considers cultural diversity
  • Helps explain group behaviour
Limitations:
  • May underestimate individual differences
  • Difficult to separate social from biological influences
  • Some concepts difficult to measure
Key Debates:
  • WEIRD samples: ~96% of psychology participants come from Western, Educated, Industrialised, Rich, Democratic countries ‚Äî limiting generalisability
  • Culture shapes biology: The culture of honour study (Cohen et al., 1996) showed cultural insults produced measurable cortisol and testosterone spikes
  • Situational determinism: Milgram and the Stanford Prison Experiment showed that social situations can overpower individual personality and conscience
  • Ethnocentrism: Tests like Ainsworth's Strange Situation impose Western norms on other cultures, producing misleading results

Level 3 Focus

Key Idea: Critical analysis of how culture and society shape psychological understanding.

Key Concepts:
  • Cultural dimensions (Hofstede): Individualism vs collectivism
  • Social constructionism: Reality is shaped by social agreement
  • Vygotsky's sociocultural theory: Cognitive development through social interaction
  • Zone of proximal development: Learning potential with guidance
Key Studies:
  • Asch conformity experiments: Line judgment study
  • Milgram obedience study: Authority and obedience
  • Cross-cultural research: Variations in psychological phenomena
Critical Evaluation:
  • Ethnocentrism in psychological research
  • Emic vs etic approaches to culture
  • Intersectionality - multiple cultural identities

Key Debates in Psychology

These four debates cut across all six approaches and are central to understanding psychology at Level 2.

Debate What It Asks Key Positions
Nature vs Nurture Is behaviour shaped by biology (genes, brain) or environment (upbringing, culture)? Modern view: interactionism ‚Äî both constantly interact. Epigenetics and the diathesis-stress model show this
Free Will vs Determinism Do we freely choose our behaviour, or is it controlled by forces beyond our control? Humanistic = free will. Biological = biological determinism. Behaviourist = environmental determinism. Psychodynamic = psychic determinism
Holism vs Reductionism Should behaviour be studied as a whole, or broken into simpler parts? Humanistic = holistic. Biological = biological reductionism. Behaviourist = environmental reductionism. Cognitive = machine reductionism
The Replication Crisis Can psychology's findings be trusted if many studies fail to replicate? Only ~36% of 100 studies replicated (2015). Causes include small samples, publication bias, and HARKing

Debates are covered in depth at Level 2. At Level 3, these debates are woven into your critical evaluation of each approach and its application to specific topics.

Flashcards

How to get the most from flashcards

Active recall: Try to answer in your head before flipping the card. Research by Karpicke & Blunt (2011) found that retrieval practice produced 50% more learning than other study methods ‚Äî even more than concept mapping.

Rate your confidence: Before each card you'll rate how sure you are. This is metacognitive monitoring ‚Äî checking what you actually know versus what you think you know. Dunlosky et al. (2013) identified self-testing and identifying weak spots as two of the most effective revision strategies.

Thinking prompts: After flipping, you'll sometimes see a "why" or "how" question. These aren't marked ‚Äî just say your answer out loud or in your head. The act of explaining is what matters. This is elaborative interrogation ‚Äî asking yourself to explain deepens encoding and connects new knowledge to what you already know (Dunlosky et al., 2013).

Quiz

Why quiz yourself?

The testing effect: Taking a quiz isn't just a way to check what you know ‚Äî it actually strengthens your memory. Roediger & Karpicke (2006) found that students who quizzed themselves remembered significantly more than those who simply re-read the material, even when total study time was the same.

Desirable difficulty: Getting a question wrong can be more valuable than getting it right. When you make an error and then learn the correct answer, the contrast creates a stronger memory trace. Bjork & Bjork (2011) call this a "desirable difficulty" ‚Äî the struggle is what makes it stick.

Go beyond these questions: The quizzes here are a solid starting point, but don't rely on them alone. Writing your own questions is one of the best ways to deepen understanding ‚Äî it forces you to think about what's actually important. Try creating your own flashcards on paper, or use a tool like Anki to build a personal deck you can review anywhere.

Brain Dump

Test your recall: pick an approach, write everything you remember, then compare with the actual summary.

Why Brain Dump?

Brain dumping is one of the most effective study techniques known. Research by Roediger & Karpicke (2006) shows that actively retrieving information from memory strengthens it far more than re-reading or highlighting.

How it works:

  1. Choose a topic below
  2. Write down everything you can remember ‚Äî don't peek!
  3. When you're done, reveal the actual summary and compare
  4. Focus future study on the gaps you find

Even if you get things wrong, the effort of trying to remember builds stronger memory traces than passive review.
